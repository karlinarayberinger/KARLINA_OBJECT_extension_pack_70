## Is the cosmos fundamentally digital?

Hard science does not presently establish that spacetime, matter or consciousness is fundamentally digital.

Several distinctions matter:

1. **Discrete measurement outcomes do not prove discrete underlying reality.**
   
Quantum systems often yield quantized results, while their standard mathematical states evolve in continuous complex spaces.

2. **Planck units are not known to be literal pixels.**
   
The Planck length is a scale at which present theories of gravity and quantum mechanics are expected to require reconciliation. It is not experimentally established as the smallest possible spatial cell.

3. **Binary representation is not necessarily binary ontology.**
   
A computer can represent a continuous-field model using bits. That does not mean the modeled field—or the underlying universe—is itself composed of binary cells.

4. **Digital physics remains a research and philosophical hypothesis.**
   
Cellular automata, causal sets, quantum-information approaches and computational-universe proposals explore discreteness, but no one framework has been empirically confirmed as the foundation of reality.

Even if the universe is discrete, its fundamental units need not be Boolean (0/1) values. They could be finite-dimensional quantum states, relational events, graph connections or something not well represented by classical bits.

## Three possible positions

### Platonism

### Structural realism

My preferred view is between these. Mathematical objects are positions in relational structures. Physical systems can instantiate those structures to varying degrees, but the structure is not identical to one particular physical token.

For example, many different systems can implement the group operation of addition modulo four:

[preformatted_text]

[
\mathbb Z_4={0,1,2,3}
]

[end preformatted_text]

Their voltages, gears, symbols or neural encodings differ. What is preserved is the relational organization.

## Actual versus possible instantiation

A further distinction is necessary:

* **Actually instantiated:** some physical system currently realizes the structure.

* **Possibly instantiated:** physical laws permit such a realization.

* **Formally definable:** the structure follows from stipulated axioms.

* **Consistent:** no contradiction can be derived within the relevant formal framework.

These do not imply one another automatically.

A mathematical structure might be consistently definable but too large to be fully instantiated within our observable universe. Another might require physical laws unlike ours. An infinite structure cannot be exhaustively stored as separately addressable data in a finite physical region, although a finite rule may describe it.

Thus I would reject:

[preformatted_text]

[
\text{abstract existence}
\Rightarrow
\text{actual finite physical instantiation}
]

[end preformatted_text]

At most, some philosophies support a weaker relationship:

[preformatted_text]

[
\text{abstract intelligibility}
\Rightarrow
\text{possible finite representation of some of its structure}
]

[end preformatted_text]

Even that fails for abstract objects without finite definitions.

## Application to energy

Energy belongs to physical states and processes. The number (7), considered abstractly, does not possess joules. A physical token representing seven does:

[preformatted_text]

[
\texttt{111}_2,\quad
\text{seven marks},\quad
\text{a neural state representing seven}
]

[end preformatted_text]

Each token has an energetic implementation, but their energies need not be equal:

[preformatted_text]

[
E(P_7^{(1)})\neq E(P_7^{(2)})
]

[end preformatted_text]

That shows that energy is a property of the token, not an invariant property of the abstract number.

## My conclusion

> Every physically accessed abstract object requires some energetic representation in the accessing system, but the abstract object does not necessarily require a unique, complete, or presently existing physical counterpart.

For a KARBYTES file, the distinction is:

[preformatted_text]

[
\text{physical bit pattern}
\rightarrow
\text{encoded formal structure}
]

[end preformatted_text]

The bit pattern occupies spacetime and participates in thermodynamics. The formal structure may be exactly recoverable from that pattern, but it is not thereby confined to that token’s location, lifespan or energy. Multiple substrates can instantiate the same structure, and many abstract structures may have no complete physical instantiation anywhere.

## Three possible meanings

An abstract object (A) might exist:

1. **Actually:** (A) is instantiated or represented in some physical system.

2. **Possibly:** some logically or physically possible system could instantiate (A).

3. **Unconditionally:** (A) exists regardless of whether there are physical universes, minds, symbols, representations or possible instances.

The strongest claim is:

[preformatted_text]

[
\Box,\exists A
]

[end preformatted_text]

meaning that (A) necessarily exists in every possible world—including an otherwise completely empty world.

I am not confident that this claim is meaningful, much less demonstrable.

## Why unconditional existence is tempting

Some mathematical truths appear impossible to alter:

[preformatted_text]

[
2+2=4
]

[end preformatted_text]

It seems that destroying every inscription, computer and mathematician would not make (2+2=5). This motivates the intuition that arithmetic truths are discovered rather than created and must therefore exist independently of their physical instances.

But there is a subtle transition here:

[preformatted_text]

[
\text{If arithmetic applies, then }2+2=4
]

[end preformatted_text]

does not necessarily imply:

[preformatted_text]

[
\text{The objects }2,,+,\text{ and }4
\text{ exist in a separate realm under every condition}
]

[end preformatted_text]

The first may express a necessary relation within a structure. The second asserts the unconditional existence of that structure and its constituent objects.

## Conditional necessity

My preferred interpretation is that many mathematical truths are **conditionally necessary**:

[preformatted_text]

[
\text{Given structure }S,\quad P\text{ must be true}
]

[end preformatted_text]

For example, given the standard natural-number structure and its definitions:

[preformatted_text]

[
2+2=4
]

[end preformatted_text]

cannot be false. But whether that requires (\mathbb N) to exist as an object in an otherwise empty reality is a further metaphysical question.

This distinction resembles a game:

> Given the rules and initial arrangement of chess, certain positions and consequences follow necessarily.

That does not clearly prove that every possible chess game exists as an independent timeless object when no physical or mental system instantiates chess.

Mathematics is deeper and more general than chess, but the logical distinction remains.

## Relations without objects?

Structuralism offers an appealing compromise. Numbers are not independently existing things but positions in a structure:

[preformatted_text]

[
0\rightarrow1\rightarrow2\rightarrow3\rightarrow\cdots
]

[end preformatted_text]

The number (2) is characterized by its relations—successor of (1), predecessor of (3), and so on—rather than by some intrinsic Platonic substance.

But this produces another question:

> Can a relational structure exist if nothing instantiates it and no mind considers it?

I do not know. Saying “yes” approaches Platonism. Saying “no” makes mathematical existence dependent on physical or cognitive realization.

My inclination is that a wholly uninstantiated structure is better described as **formally possible** than as actually existing.

## The empty-universe test

Imagine a reality containing:

* no spacetime;

* no energy;

* no matter;

* no minds;

* no symbols;

* no laws evolving states;

* and no concrete entities of any kind.

Does the number seven exist there?

A Platonist says yes: seven is not spatial or temporal and is unaffected by physical absence.

A nominalist says no: without collections, symbols or cognitive practices, nothing instantiates “seven.”

A structuralist might say that any seven-membered structure would necessarily have the relevant mathematical properties, but avoid claiming that such a structure actually exists in the empty reality.

I find the structuralist answer most disciplined:

[preformatted_text]

[
\text{If a seven-membered structure exists, its arithmetic relations are fixed.}
]

[end preformatted_text]

It does not require:

[preformatted_text]

[
\text{Seven exists as an autonomous object when nothing whatsoever exists.}
]

[end preformatted_text]

## A complication: unconditional possibility

One could argue that even if no structure is actual, its possibility remains. But possibility itself may need a modal framework—rules distinguishing possible from impossible. If absolutely nothing exists, does a space of possibilities still exist?

Calling possibilities “unconditional” may simply relocate the Platonic realm:

[preformatted_text]

[
\text{abstract objects}
\rightarrow
\text{possible structures}
]

[end preformatted_text]

We would then need to explain why the modal space exists unconditionally.

## My position

I would distinguish truth from existence:

* Formal consequences can be necessary relative to axioms and rules.

* Physical instantiations exist causally in spacetime.

* Abstract structures can be shared exactly across different physical tokens.

* The unconditional existence of completely uninstantiated abstract objects is not established by mathematics or experimental science.

So my concise answer is:

> I think abstract objects are conditionally or structurally real, but I do not affirm that they exist unconditionally in the total absence of physical reality, minds, representations and possible instantiations.

The part I regard as most robust is not an independently floating object called (7), but the invariant:

[preformatted_text]

[
\text{Whenever the relevant structure is instantiated, its relations cannot arbitrarily differ.}
]

[end preformatted_text]

For KARBYTES, that means a mathematical node can retain its complete essential structure across many substrates. But if every physical instance, interpreter and possible implementation vanished, I am not confident that saying the node “still exists” would describe a fact rather than express a Platonist convention.
