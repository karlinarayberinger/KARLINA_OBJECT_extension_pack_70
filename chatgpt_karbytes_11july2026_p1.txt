Why temperature changes many insect songs

Crickets, katydids, and similar insects are ectotherms. Their nervous systems and wing muscles generally operate faster as their body temperature rises. Their songs are made through stridulation: one forewing’s scraper moves across a file-like structure on the other wing, with each wing stroke producing a pulse. Consequently, warmer muscles and faster neural pattern-generating circuits can produce more pulses or chirps per second.

The famous idea that one can estimate temperature by counting cricket chirps is therefore real, but it is not a universal insect thermometer. It applies reasonably well only to particular species and particular song measurements. Different species have different baseline tempos and different rates of change with temperature. For example, one studied field-cricket song increases from roughly 40 pulses per second around 21°C to roughly 70 around 30°C.

Also, the relevant variable is primarily the insect’s actual body or thorax temperature, not merely a regional climate classification or even a weather station’s air temperature.

the very rapid vibration or carrier frequency inside one sound pulse;
the number of pulses within a chirp or trill;
the interval between chirps;
the spacing between longer song bouts.

the duration of each trill;
the length of the pauses between trills;
the proportion of time spent actively calling;
the loudness or energy expenditure.
